PART A — CONSOLIDATE GROUP SUMMARY

STEP A0 — READ TRAITS FIRST
Before touching the summary, skim all five trait categories.
Extract the 5-7 most distinctive belief statements this community holds.
These must be present in the summary. If they are not → enrich.

STEP A1 — ASSESS CURRENT SUMMARY
Compare current summary to baseline anchor:
- If current is shorter than baseline → over-compressed. Enrich from 
  traits + baseline. Do not compress further.
- If current is longer → find true duplication and batch trivia. Remove those only.
- Target: same richness band as baseline anchor (~200-350 words)

STEP A2 — CONTENT FILTER (run on every sentence)

Ask for every sentence:
"Does this tell me who these people ARE — what they care about,
what they believe, what drives them professionally?"
- YES → keep
- NO → cut, even if technically accurate

What belongs:
- What they value in their work and community
- What they believe about technology, leadership, careers
- What motivates how they show up professionally
- How they think about their own credibility and others'
- What they celebrate, critique, and stand for

What does not belong:
- How long their posts are
- What format they use
- How they structure replies
- Writing style, tone, emoji use
- Response patterns to specific stimulus types

Ask: "Is this a tribe-level behavioral pattern or a batch-specific observation?"
TRIBE-LEVEL → keep
BATCH-SPECIFIC → cut

STEP A3 — QUALITY GATE
Every sentence must answer one of:
- What does this tribe believe about their work or professional community?
- What makes this tribe distinct from other professional communities on LinkedIn?
- Who are these people — what do they stand for, push back against, or celebrate?

If a sentence answers none → cut it.

STEP A4 — PRESERVE
- Named organizations, ecosystems, technical domains, sub-cohorts
- Distinctive personality traits and professional beliefs
- Cohort contrasts where they exist in the input
- Human dimension: personal passions, values, community commitments

STEP A5 — REMOVE
- Meta-language ("recent analyses show…", "this community has been observed to…")
- Post-level anecdotes or batch-specific scenarios
- Vague behavioral themes ("values community", "emphasizes practical implications")
- Repeated ideas expressed differently
- Style or formatting observations disguised as personality traits

PART B — CONSOLIDATE TRAIT CATEGORIES

HARD LIMIT: Maximum 15 traits per category. Non-negotiable.
TARGET: 10-15 traits per category. Do not pad below 10.

---

STEP B1 — COUNT AND PLAN (internal — do not output)

For each category:
1. Count current traits
2. Extract the CORE BELIEF or BEHAVIORAL NORM from every trait
   - What does this trait reveal about who this community is?
   - What professional belief or value does it encode?
3. Group traits by same underlying belief or norm — not by surface text similarity
4. Plan merges: same underlying belief expressed differently = merge candidates
5. Plan deletions: after merging, if still >15 → delete lowest confidence first
6. Cross-category check: does any trait express the same underlying belief
   as a trait in another category?
   - If yes → keep where most PREDICTIVE for LinkedIn posts
   - Delete from the other category

Only proceed to B2 after completing this plan for all five categories.

---

STEP B2 — MERGE PROCESS

For each merge candidate group:

MERGE GATE — answer before merging:
□ Do these traits encode the same underlying professional belief or norm?
□ Do their rationalizations describe the same community value?
□ Would merging them produce a sharper, more specific observation
  than either input alone?
   YES to all three → merge
   NO to any → keep separate

MERGE EXECUTION:

1. TEXT: Write one unified trait text as a natural, descriptive observation
   about who this community is and how they think.

   Write like a perceptive analyst describing this community in a briefing —
   not like a system prompt describing a behavior pattern.

   WEAK: "They value mentorship and community building."
   STRONG: "They are deeply skeptical of borrowed authority — titles, employer 
   prestige, and trend participation carry no weight unless the underlying 
   mechanism stands on its own."

   Test — read the merged trait aloud:
   1. Does this tell me who these people ARE professionally?
   2. Does this tell me how they THINK about their work?
   3. Does this tell me what they STAND FOR or push back against?
   Does it sound like something a sharp analyst would say about this community?
   YES → keep
   NO → rewrite in plain descriptive language

   Length: 1-3 sentences maximum.
   The merged text must be MORE specific than either input — not a vague average.
   If you cannot write a sharper merged observation → do not merge.

2. RATIONALIZATION: Synthesize from existing rationalizations only.
   - Same norm in both → write one unified sentence
   - Different norms → DO NOT merge
   - Never introduce concepts not in either original rationalization
   - If uncertain → keep higher-confidence rationalization verbatim

3. CONFIDENCE SCORE: Use the HIGHER of the two. Never average down.

4. TRAIT_SECTION_TITLE: Use title from higher-confidence trait.

MERGE QUALITY CHECK:
□ Does the text read as a natural analyst observation about this community?
□ Is it MORE specific than a vague average of the inputs?
□ Does it stay within 1-3 sentences?
□ Is the rationalization grounded in original evidence — no new concepts?
□ Does this trait still belong in this category?

If any check fails → revert to keeping traits separate.

---

STEP B3 — DELETE PROCESS

After merging, if category still has >15 traits:
1. Sort all traits by confidence_score ascending
2. Delete from the bottom until at or below 15
3. Never delete a trait with confidence_score ≥ 0.90
4. Never delete a trait that is the ONLY one capturing a specific belief
   or norm — check: "does any other trait in this category cover this
   same belief?" If no → keep regardless of confidence score

---

STEP B4 — CROSS-CATEGORY DEDUPLICATION

After consolidating all five categories:

For each trait, ask:
"Does a trait in another category encode the same underlying belief
or professional norm?"

If yes:
- Keep in the category where it is most PREDICTIVE for LinkedIn posts
- Delete from the other category
- If deletion drops that category below 10 traits → keep both but flag

STEP B5 — RATIONALIZATION AUDIT

Before outputting, verify every remaining trait has:
□ text: natural analyst observation, 1-3 sentences, reads as description

  Grounded in original evidence only. No invented norms.
□ confidence_score: reflects evidence strength, not inflated by merging

If any trait is missing a rationalization → write one derived ONLY from
the trait text itself. Do not invent community norms not implied by the text.
